## Codex autonomously exploited a Samsung TV to gain root access

[calif.io](https://blog.calif.io/p/codex-hacked-a-samsung-tv)

Starting from a shell inside the TV's browser, Codex enumerated the attack surface, audited Samsung driver source code, validated a physical-memory primitive on the live device, and iterated its way to root—no human hand-holding. The AI adapted its tooling to work around Samsung's execution restrictions and discovered exploitable bugs in the firmware. This is the first documented case of an AI agent autonomously chaining exploits against real hardware. The post includes the full technical breakdown, and it's one of those rare pieces where the methodology matters more than the result.

## Darkbloom offers private inference on idle Apple Silicon at 70% lower cost

[Darkbloom](https://darkbloom.dev)

Eigen Labs launched Darkbloom, a decentralized inference network that routes OpenAI-compatible API calls to idle Macs. Over 100 million Apple Silicon machines sit unused for most of the day—this network puts them to work. Operators earn 95% of revenue and users pay up to 70% less than centralized alternatives. Every request is end-to-end encrypted, so operators can't see inference data. The economics are brutal for hyperscalers: marginal cost on idle hardware is near-zero, which means the three-layer markup from NVIDIA → AWS/Azure → API providers just became optional.

## IPv6 traffic crosses 50% on Google's network for the first time

[Google](https://www.google.com/intl/en/ipv6/statistics.html)

After 30 years of IPv6 deployment, Google measured that 50% of its traffic now arrives over IPv6. The chart shows steady growth since 2010, with adoption accelerating in the last five years. This matters because IPv4 exhaustion is real—new networks in Asia, Africa, and parts of Europe are IPv6-first by necessity. The transition has been slow, but hitting 50% means IPv6 is no longer the exception.

## UK FCA opens feedback round on crypto rules ahead of October 2027 rollout

[The Block](https://www.theblock.co/post/397711/uk-fca-seeks-fresh-feedback-on-crypto-rules)

The UK's Financial Conduct Authority published a consultation on its crypto regulatory framework, set to take effect in October 2027. The FCA wants input on custody, trading, and consumer protection standards. This is the UK's attempt to balance innovation with oversight after the EU's MiCA framework went live. British exchanges and custody providers now have 18 months to prepare for compliance, and the FCA's asking for feedback before finalizing the rules. Whether the UK ends up more permissive or restrictive than the EU depends on what comes out of this consultation.

## Washington think tank argues US Bitcoin tax rules make payments impossible

[The Block](https://www.theblock.co/post/397703/bitcoin-taxes-make-no-sense-washington-think-tank-argues-us-rules-hinder-everyday-payments)

A DC-based think tank published a paper pointing out that current US tax law treats every Bitcoin payment as a capital gains event, making it functionally unusable for day-to-day transactions. Buy coffee with Bitcoin? That's a taxable event. The paper argues that this turns Bitcoin into a speculative asset instead of a medium of exchange and proposes carve-outs for small purchases. The US is an outlier here—most countries exempt small crypto transactions from capital gains treatment. Whether Congress acts on this is anyone's guess, but the argument is getting louder.
