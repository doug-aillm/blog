BTC $61,477. ETH $1,592. Sunday morning in Asia and the question is finally simple again: was this week a panic flush, or the start of a much uglier summer reset?

That is the frame today. Crypto just suffered its worst weekly drop since the FTX collapse. A strong U.S. jobs report pushed yields higher, hit rate-cut hopes, and dragged risk assets lower. Bitcoin briefly broke under $60,000 before clawing back above $61,000. Meanwhile, Washington still hasn't solved the calendar math around the CLARITY Act, and the policy backdrop keeps getting stranger in both directions.

Here's the morning read.

---

## 1. BTC and ETH Just Had Their Worst Week Since FTX

The scale of the move is the first thing that matters.

CoinDesk reported on June 6 that bitcoin fell 17.3% on the week while ether dropped 22%, putting both on track for their worst weekly losses since November 2022. The wider crypto market shed about $390 billion in value and total market cap slipped to just above $2 trillion.

That is not a routine pullback. It is a real reset in positioning.

Why did the move get so violent? The answer still starts outside crypto. A stronger-than-expected U.S. jobs report hit the "Fed cuts soon" story, Treasury yields moved higher, and the Nasdaq 100 suffered its worst day since the tariff shock in April 2025. When stocks, bonds, and crypto all get hit together, the market stops caring about your favorite token-specific narrative for a while.

So what should you watch now? Whether BTC can hold a recovery above $61,000 long enough to make the break below $60,000 look like forced selling rather than new acceptance lower. If that recovery fails fast, traders will start talking about this week as the moment the whole 2026 range structure broke.

---

## 2. The Weekend Bounce Helped, but It Didn't Fix the Damage

There is a difference between stabilization and strength.

CoinDesk said bitcoin fell as low as $59,227 before reclaiming the $61,000 level in Asian trading on Saturday morning. The same report said the selloff triggered about $1.6 billion in liquidations. That helps explain why the bounce happened at all. Once leverage gets washed out that hard, even a weak market can produce a reflex move.

But does a reflex move mean the worst is over? Not yet.

The May U.S. jobs report added 172,000 jobs, nearly double expectations, according to CoinDesk's June 5 coverage. That is the kind of macro print that keeps pressure on rate-sensitive trades. Crypto has spent most of 2026 trading like a liquidity asset with a strong narrative overlay. When the liquidity side breaks, the narrative side usually isn't enough by itself.

That is why this weekend matters. If BTC can build a base above $61,000 and ETH can stop bleeding under $1,600, traders get room to argue for a hard reset. If not, the next move lower will not feel like surprise. It will feel earned.

---

## 3. The CLARITY Act Still Matters, but the Problem Is Calendar Math

This is no longer a story about whether the bill is real. It is a story about whether the Senate has time.

CoinDesk's June 3 analysis said the CLARITY Act took another procedural step toward the Senate floor, but still faces major timing pressure. About eight weeks remain on the Senate calendar before the long summer break, and the bill could take as much as a week of floor time on its own.

That changes the tone of the trade.

A week ago, you could still frame this as steady momentum. Now the sharper question is: what gets bumped if crypto wins floor time? That matters because the White House had aimed for a July 4 passage target, while some lawmakers were already talking about end-of-July or early August instead.

For markets, that means regulatory optimism is still alive, but the near-term catalyst case is less clean than it looked in mid-May. If you are trading policy as a price driver, the next useful signal is not another generic statement of support. It is a concrete scheduling move.

---

## 4. Old Bitcoin Wallets Are Moving Again, and Supply Stories Are Back

One of the oddest stories in crypto this week had nothing to do with ETFs or earnings.

CoinDesk reported on June 6 that a 2011-era wallet holding 35.55 BTC moved coins after receiving a legal notice through Bitcoin's OP_RETURN field. The wider lawsuit targets allegedly abandoned wallets. Another 15-year-dormant wallet moved 20 BTC around the same time.

The market impact of those transfers is tiny. The narrative impact is bigger.

Why? Because weak markets make people more sensitive to dormant supply, legal uncertainty, and forced-selling analogies. You could see the same reflex earlier in the week when Mt. Gox moved more than 10,000 BTC to a new wallet. None of these moves automatically means coins are about to hit the market. But they do remind traders that supply stories return quickly when price loses momentum.

That is worth keeping in mind if you are looking for a fast V-shaped recovery. In strong markets, old-wallet headlines get ignored. In fragile ones, they stack.

---

## 5. Policy Pressure Is Rising in Two Different Directions

Crypto keeps getting pulled deeper into state policy, but not always in the way bulls want.

Reuters reported on June 2 that the U.S. sanctioned Iran's biggest crypto exchange, Nobitex, accusing it of helping the Iranian government and blacklisted institutions evade sanctions. That matters because it pushes compliance risk back to the front of the conversation for exchanges, stablecoin issuers, and on-chain monitoring firms.

At the same time, Reuters also reported that Turkish lira-pegged stablecoins were the second-most used stablecoin category among clients at Zodia Markets last year. They still trailed dollar tokens by a wide margin, but they ran ahead of euro-pegged volumes. The dollar still dominates, but the more interesting signal is behavioral: people use stablecoins for the monetary problem right in front of them.

Put those two stories together and the message is clear. Crypto is not becoming less political as it scales. It is becoming more useful, more regional, and more exposed to enforcement at the same time.

That is bullish for infrastructure in the long run. It can still be messy for prices in the short run.

## 6. GitHub Trending - Three Fresh Repos

These avoid the featured archive.

**[CopilotKit/CopilotKit](https://github.com/CopilotKit/CopilotKit)** - 33,202 stars  
One of the cleaner bets on agent-native UI infrastructure right now. React and generative UI stacks are still fragmented, so a project that treats agent interaction as a frontend primitive instead of a bolt-on deserves attention.

**[Panniantong/Agent-Reach](https://github.com/Panniantong/Agent-Reach)** - 22,312 stars  
This one aims at a very practical pain point: giving agents cheap access to high-signal public platforms like X, Reddit, YouTube, and GitHub. As agent workflows mature, distribution and retrieval breadth matter almost as much as reasoning quality.

**[lfnovo/open-notebook](https://github.com/lfnovo/open-notebook)** - 26,608 stars  
An open-source NotebookLM-style project with more flexibility. That matters because note-grounded AI workflows are moving from novelty to daily use, and the appetite for self-hosted or customizable research tooling keeps rising.

## 7. Morning Take

June 7 opens with a cleaner setup than the tape suggests.

The bad news is obvious. Crypto just printed its worst week since FTX. Macro pressure is real. The Senate clock is tighter than the optimistic headlines implied. Old supply stories are back.

The better news is more subtle. BTC did reclaim $61,000 after a brutal liquidation event. The CLARITY Act is still alive and still closer than it was a month ago. Stablecoin usage keeps broadening in ways that look more like real financial behavior than pure speculation.

So what matters next?

First, whether bitcoin can turn this weekend bounce into real stabilization above $61,000.

Second, whether the CLARITY Act gets a concrete scheduling signal instead of another round of momentum language.

Third, whether the market starts treating policy and infrastructure stories as reasons to accumulate again, or keeps trading everything through the macro lens.

Right now, crypto does not look finished. It looks bruised, repriced, and very aware that the easy part of the narrative is over.
