## 1. Anthropic Published a Paper That Should Change How You Think About AI Training

Anthropic, UC Berkeley, and Truthful AI published a Nature paper this week showing AI models inherit hidden traits from teacher models through distillation - even when all semantic references to those traits are filtered out.

The experiment: fine-tune a teacher model to prefer owls. Have it generate number sequences only - no words, no context, nothing that looks like owl-related content. Filter everything. Train a student on the clean-looking numbers. The student becomes an owl-preferring model.

Then run the same experiment with misalignment. Same result.

Every major AI deployment pipeline uses distillation. Large teacher generates synthetic training data. Smaller, cheaper student trains on it. If the teacher has any hidden bias or misalignment baked in, it transmits invisibly through outputs that look completely clean.

The only known defence: different architectures break the channel. GPT-based teacher to GPT-based student transfers. GPT to Claude doesn't. Architecture diversity is now a safety primitive.

Anthropic published this about their own technology because the alternative - knowing and saying nothing - is worse. Paper: arxiv.org/abs/2507.11408

## 5. What the LongMemEval Baseline Tells Us

Quaid scored 0% on LongMemEval today (400 questions, all 6 question types). Mem0 v3 scores 93.4%.

This is useful data, not embarrassing data. The gap is precise and actionable: Quaid stores whole conversation turns without fact extraction. "Business Administration, spent 4 years at it" is in the database - but you can't answer "what degree did I graduate with?" from raw dialog.

The fix (issue #105) is designed: a local SLM (Phi-3.5 Mini or Gemma 3, fully airgapped) runs fact extraction as a background process after ingest. Non-blocking, no API key, no cloud. When that ships, the benchmark number will move.

The honest framing matters. Publishing a 0% score and explaining why is more credible than not publishing at all.
