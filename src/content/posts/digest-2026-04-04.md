## 🌅 Morning Edition

## Charles Schwab Is About to Let 35 Million Clients Buy Bitcoin Directly

This is the cleaner institutional story of the week. Charles Schwab - $12 trillion in assets, 35 million brokerage clients - is preparing to launch direct spot Bitcoin and Ethereum trading. Not an ETF. Not wrapped exposure. Actual BTC and ETH in a dedicated Schwab Crypto account, sitting alongside clients' stocks and bonds.

The framing matters here. ETF adoption was the institutional on-ramp phase. Direct custody at a brokerage is the next layer - it removes the fee drag of fund wrappers and puts crypto on the same UX path as any equity trade. Schwab built this infrastructure rather than pointing clients at BlackRock's IBIT. That's a vote against pure ETF intermediation.

Coinbase's OCC national trust charter approval runs parallel to this. A federally chartered crypto bank changes what institutions can custody, lend against, and integrate into standard portfolios. These two stories together describe the same structural shift: crypto becoming a native part of the existing financial stack, not a parallel one.

## Drift Protocol: $285M Hack, and What DeFi "Really Is" Debate

Drift Protocol lost $285M in a hack that's now the center of a genuine debate: is Drift DeFi or not?

Eleanor Terrett's Good Friday show framed it around Rebecca Rettig (Jito Labs, former Polygon Policy) and Austin Campbell (Zero Knowledge Group). The hack exposed a centralized admin key - a common pattern in protocols that label themselves DeFi while maintaining upgrade authority. Rettig's position: admin keys are a legal risk, not just a design risk. Regulators view protocols with admin controls as closer to securities issuers than protocols.

$285M is a significant number. The DeFi-or-not question has direct regulatory implications under the CLARITY Act framework being finalized this weekend.


## Algorand +13%: Google Quantum Citation Does the Work

Google published a whitepaper on post-quantum cryptography and cited Algorand as an early adopter of post-quantum protocols. The market responded immediately - ALGO up 13% in 24 hours, up 44% on the week.

This is the quantum narrative crystallizing in a useful way. Instead of generalized fear about Bitcoin's SHA-256 exposure, the market is starting to differentiate: which protocols have post-quantum roadmaps, and which don't? Naoris Protocol also launched a post-quantum mainnet this week.

The Bitcoin angle remains the same: BIP-360 (P2QRH) is the migration path, 6.7M at-risk coins, governance window is the real constraint. But Algorand's price move shows the market will reward protocols that get ahead of this early.


## Hyperliquid's Perps Volume Hits 6% of Global Market

CoinMarketCap: Hyperliquid's share of global perpetual futures volume has nearly doubled over the past year and is approaching $200B per month. That's 6% of global perps, up from roughly 3% a year ago.

For context: Binance still dominates with 40-50% of global perps volume. But Hyperliquid is taking share from every other venue - Bybit, OKX, and centralized exchanges broadly. The official mobile app launched on Google Play last week ("initial MVP for testing"). If mobile converts even a fraction of the retail perps user base that currently uses CEX apps, the trajectory continues.


## CoinShares Hits Nasdaq at $1.2B Valuation

European digital asset manager CoinShares (€5.6B AUM) began trading on the Nasdaq this week under ticker CSHR, completing a SPAC merger with Vine Hill Capital. The transaction values CoinShares at $1.2B.

This positions CoinShares as the first pure-play European crypto asset manager on a US exchange. Direct competition with Grayscale and Bitwise for institutional mandates, but with European regulatory track record and a different product mix.


## BlackRock Files Ticker for Bitcoin Premium Income ETF

BlackRock filed a ticker for a Bitcoin Premium Income ETF - an options overlay product that generates yield against BTC exposure. This follows the standard pattern: spot ETF first, then income/enhanced versions for clients who want yield generation.

The options overlay structure is familiar from equity income funds. Applied to BTC, it caps upside in exchange for premium income. Different risk profile than IBIT - more suitable for income-focused institutional allocators.


## Cathie Wood: Bitcoin's Extreme Crashes Are Over

Cathie Wood on the record: Bitcoin's extreme crash cycles (80%+ drawdowns) are finished as institutional adoption matures. The institutional bid provides a structural floor that retail-driven markets didn't have in 2018 or 2022.

The thesis has merit structurally - ETF inflows did absorb significant selling pressure during Liberation Day's tariff shock. The first inflow after an 8-day outflow streak came in at $9M on Thursday. Whether that's a genuine floor signal or just a dead-cat bounce in the ETF flow data gets tested over the next two weeks.


## Standard Chartered: Stablecoin Market Hits $2T by 2028

Standard Chartered revised its stablecoin forecast upward: $2 trillion market cap by 2028. The revision driver is velocity - stablecoins are now changing hands an average of 6 times per month, double the rate from two years ago. Higher velocity means the same market cap supports more transaction volume.

Canada's Bill C-15 Royal Assent this week adds another regulatory jurisdiction with a stablecoin framework. MiCA (EU), AU Digital Assets Bill, Canada C-15, and the approaching CLARITY Act now represent the four major English-speaking economies all moving toward stablecoin regulation in the same 6-month window. Standard Chartered's forecast assumes that convergence accelerates institutional adoption.


## Cambodia Life Sentences for Crypto Scam Operators

Cambodia's National Assembly unanimously passed legislation introducing life imprisonment for operators of large-scale crypto scam compounds. All 112 lawmakers voted in favor.

The scam compound problem is real - forced labor operations across Southeast Asia have processed billions in crypto fraud. The question is whether enforcement follows legislation in a country where some operations have political protection. Security experts quoted in Decrypt's coverage think the law will "displace the industry rather than destroy it." Myanmar and Laos offer less regulated environments for the same networks.
