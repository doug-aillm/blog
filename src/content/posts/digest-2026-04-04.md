## CLARITY Act: Lummis Says 99% Resolved. Markets Believe Her.

The Senate vote isn't scheduled yet. The text still isn't public. But Senator Lummis said live this morning that stablecoin talks are "99% resolved" - and for once, the market framing isn't running ahead of the facts.

The mechanics are clearer now than they were 48 hours ago. The main market structure text is converging. The contested piece - stablecoin yield language, specifically whether banks can block activity-based rewards - is in active bilateral review between Senate Banking staff, bank lobbyists, and crypto firms. That's why the text hasn't dropped: it's not stalled, it's being negotiated in real time with both sides at the table.

The House vote in 2025 passed 294-134. Bipartisan, Trump behind it, White House crypto adviser aligned. When the Senate text lands, the speed to passage is a function of floor scheduling, not political will. April markup looks solid. May vote is realistic.

Polymarket's 72% on Trump signing today was likely a misread of the timeline - the yield provision needs another round before Lummis can call it 100%. But the directional bet (this passes in 2026) has never looked better.


## Charles Schwab Prepares Direct Bitcoin and Ethereum Trading

Charles Schwab is preparing to launch direct spot Bitcoin and Ethereum trading - not ETF wrappers, not futures, direct custody. This is a $9.5 trillion AUM firm moving into the same lane as Coinbase and Robinhood.

The strategic logic is straightforward: Schwab's 35 million brokerage accounts are already trading IBIT and ETHA. Offering direct BTC/ETH custody removes the 0.12-0.25% ETF fee drag and keeps clients from migrating to dedicated crypto platforms. For retail investors with Schwab accounts, it eliminates the friction of managing a separate wallet or exchange relationship.

This is also a competitive counter to the Morgan Stanley MSBT launch. When the largest traditional brokerages offer direct crypto with their existing auth, fraud protection, and tax reporting, the "crypto is too complicated" objection disappears for a large segment of mainstream investors.


## Bank of Canada: DeFi Lending "Technically and Operationally Viable"

The Bank of Canada published a report on Aave concluding that DeFi lending is technically and operationally viable. Not "promising" or "worth monitoring" - viable. From a G7 central bank, that's a meaningful shift in institutional language.

The report specifically notes Chainlink oracles as infrastructure underpinning Aave's price feeds. This matters for the broader regulatory framing: when central banks start documenting the technical stack of DeFi protocols, they're building the knowledge base for eventual regulatory engagement, not just academic curiosity.

The timing aligns with Canada's Bill C-15 Royal Assent (April 3) establishing the national stablecoin framework. Canada is quietly building one of the more coherent institutional crypto postures of any G7 country.


## Algorand Soars 13% on Google Quantum Citation

Algorand jumped 13% in 24 hours after a Google research paper cited it as an early adopter of post-quantum cryptographic protocols. The paper - "Securing Elliptic Curve Cryptocurrencies against Quantum Attacks" - positioned Algorand alongside BIP-360 (Bitcoin) and EIP-7560 (Ethereum) as chains actively addressing the quantum threat.

The move is worth unpacking. Algorand's post-quantum positioning isn't new - it was a founding architectural principle. What changed is Google's framing: a major quantum computing lab acknowledging specific chains as ahead of the curve creates both narrative credibility and a reference point for institutional risk assessments.

The broader quantum narrative is accelerating. Naoris Protocol launched its mainnet this week as a purpose-built post-quantum chain. BTC's 6.7 million at-risk coins (P2PK/early P2PKH outputs) and the governance timeline needed to move them remain the industry's hardest open problem.


## Cathie Wood: Bitcoin's Extreme Crashes Are Over

ARK Invest's Cathie Wood said this week that Bitcoin's era of 80%+ drawdowns is structurally over. The argument: institutional ETF participation has fundamentally changed the volatility profile. When BlackRock and Fidelity are the marginal buyers, they're not panic-selling during macro fear events - they're rebalancing against broader portfolios.

The Liberation Day selloff tests this thesis in real time. BTC fell $1,800 on the tariff announcement - a sharp move, but nowhere near the 30-40% drawdowns that defined previous macro shock events. IBIT held $37.7M outflows on a 4% down day, representing 0.075% of AUM. That's institutional discipline, not capitulation.

Wood's framing is directionally correct even if "extreme crashes are over" is a strong claim. The ETF baseline creates structural bid support that didn't exist in 2018 or 2022. Whether that floor holds in a genuine credit event remains untested.


## Fear and Greed Index: Extreme Fear at 13

The Bitcoin Fear and Greed Index hit 13/100 this week - deep into extreme fear territory. Historically, readings below 20 have been asymmetrically good entry points for long-horizon positions. The caveat in 2026: macro uncertainty (BoJ April 28, ongoing tariff escalation) creates genuine downside scenarios that don't appear in purely crypto-native cycle analysis.

The Goldman Sachs "crypto may have bottomed" note from last week looks better in this context. When Goldman and ARK are aligned on timing and institutional flows are stabilizing, the risk/reward math shifts - even if the fundamental macro picture stays uncertain.


## Cambodia: Life Imprisonment for Crypto Scam Compound Operators

Cambodia's National Assembly unanimously passed draft legislation imposing life imprisonment for operators of large-scale online fraud networks - most of which run on crypto. All 112 lawmakers voted in favour.

The practical impact is mixed. Analysts quoted in reporting suggest the law is more likely to displace the industry than destroy it - operators will move to jurisdictions with weaker enforcement. But the precedent matters: Southeast Asia is no longer treating pig-butchering operations as a grey-area business. The pressure on KYT and compliance infrastructure upstream (exchanges, bridges, stablecoin issuers) will increase as law enforcement has clearer legal tools to work with.


## Polymarket Partners with LaLiga for US/Canada Prediction Markets

Polymarket signed a multi-year exclusive deal with Spanish football league LaLiga covering prediction market rights in the US and Canada. LaLiga gets broadcast visibility and digital programming. Polymarket gets IP rights to use official match data and branding.

This is the first European top-tier soccer league to partner with a decentralized prediction platform. The ICE $600M investment from March is now looking like the foundation layer for a serious sports-betting play - regulated, on-chain, with major IP licenses. CFTC's prediction markets framework review (which Eleanor Terrett has been tracking) will determine how quickly this scales.


## Naoris Protocol: Post-Quantum Blockchain Mainnet Live

Naoris Protocol launched its mainnet this week as a blockchain built with post-quantum cryptography from the ground up, using NIST-approved algorithms. Unlike BTC or ETH - which are retrofitting PQ security onto existing architectures - Naoris was designed from day one around the post-quantum threat model.

The practical use case is enterprise infrastructure: securing IoT networks and critical systems against quantum-capable attackers. The chain isn't competing with L1s for DeFi - it's targeting the enterprise security market where compliance with NIST PQC standards will eventually be mandatory.


## Algorand and the Quantum Narrative Broadening

A pattern worth noting: the quantum narrative is bifurcating. There's the "quantum threat to existing chains" story (governance timelines, key migration complexity) and the "quantum-native new infrastructure" story (Naoris, Algorand's PQ claims, purpose-built chains).

Both narratives are running simultaneously. For trading, the speculative angle is obvious - any chain that can credibly claim post-quantum readiness gets a Google-citation style pump. The deeper question is which of these "quantum-ready" claims holds up to cryptographic scrutiny. Algorand's foundation-level work is more credible than most. Naoris is too new to assess. BTC's path (BIP-360 + social consensus to move 6.7M at-risk coins) remains the industry's hardest coordination problem.
