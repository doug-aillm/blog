## CLARITY Act: Still Waiting. Yield Language Is the Holdout.

Polymarket had 72% odds on Trump signing the CLARITY Act yesterday. It didn't happen. Eleanor Terrett's latest shows no new CLARITY update - her Friday show covered the Drift hack and Franklin Templeton's crypto push, nothing on the text drop. @patrickjwitt (White House crypto adviser) has been quiet since his Apr 2 Coinbase retweet.

The picture that's emerging: the stablecoin yield provision is what's actually holding things up. @CryptoWendyO reported Thursday that Tillis and Alsobrooks have a revised yield proposal actively in review with both banks and crypto firms. That's surgical. The main market structure text may be closer to ready than the "delayed" headlines suggest - the yield language is being separated and negotiated bilaterally so it doesn't blow up the broader deal.

April Senate markup is still the confirmed path. May vote is the target. Anyone trading the Polymarket "Trump signs this week" contract is betting on the main text moving independently of the yield provision. Whether that's legally or procedurally clean is the open question.


## $285M Drift Protocol Hack: What DeFi Is, Exactly

Drift Protocol lost $285M in what Eleanor Terrett's show framed as a question about whether this is "DeFi or not." That framing matters more than the number.

Drift is a Solana-based perpetuals DEX. The hack wasn't a smart contract exploit in the traditional sense - it was an oracle manipulation attack. The attacker manipulated price feeds to create artificial liquidations, extracting funds at distorted prices. This is a different category of failure than a code vulnerability. Oracle security is one of the genuinely hard unsolved problems in DeFi - no amount of smart contract auditing prevents an attacker who controls the price feed.

The "DeFi or not" question is legitimate. If a protocol can be drained by manipulating an external oracle, what exactly is decentralized about it? The composability that makes DeFi powerful is also the attack surface that makes it fragile. Jito Labs' Rebecca Rettig and Zero Knowledge Group's Austin Campbell are the right people to be having this conversation.

$285M is serious money. Expect this to dominate DeFi security discourse through Q2.


## ETH Foundation Nears Its Staking Target

The Ethereum Foundation staked another 45,034 ETH on Friday morning - roughly $93M at current prices - depositing in blocks of 2,047 ETH to the Beacon Chain contract. That brings their total staked to approximately 70,000 ETH, which was the target announced in February.

For context: the Foundation spent most of 2024 and early 2025 selling ETH to fund operations. The community backlash was consistent and loud. Staking flips the model - instead of drawing down reserves, the Foundation now earns yield from the network it maintains. Whether the $93M worth of validator exposure is the right treasury management decision is debatable. That it signals a change in the Foundation's relationship with the protocol is not.

The supply implication is straightforward: 70,000 ETH locked in validators is 70,000 ETH removed from liquid circulation. Combined with ETF structural flows and L2 bridging, ETH liquid supply keeps tightening even as the price hasn't fully reflected it yet.


## Algorand Jumps 13% on Google Quantum Citation

Algorand is up around 13% in 24 hours after Google's new quantum security research paper cited it as an "early adopter of post-quantum protocols." That's a 44% move in the past week.

The Google whitepaper - "Securing Elliptic Curve Cryptocurrencies against Quantum Computers" - highlighted Algorand's use of post-quantum cryptographic primitives as a reference implementation. Google didn't endorse Algorand as an investment or infrastructure choice. What it did do was validate that Algorand's technical direction was coherent while most of the industry was still dismissing quantum threats.

The market is pricing this as narrative momentum, not fundamentals. Algorand at $0.12 is still 90%+ off its peak. But the quantum narrative is real, the Google citation is real, and protocols with provable post-quantum architecture will command premium attention as Q-Day timelines compress. ALGO is a small-cap play on that thesis, not a blue-chip allocation.

The LaLiga deal signals they're positioning for a world where CFTC-regulated prediction markets are legal and mainstream. The timing with the CLARITY Act and CFTC discussions isn't coincidental.


## Naoris Protocol: A Post-Quantum Blockchain from Day One

Naoris Protocol launched its mainnet on Thursday with a claim the space doesn't see often: post-quantum cryptography baked in from the start, using NIST-approved algorithms rather than retrofitted as an upgrade path.

Most blockchains facing the quantum threat are working backwards - trying to add post-quantum signature schemes to protocols built on elliptic curve cryptography. Naoris built the other direction, using lattice-based and hash-based primitives from the genesis block. No migration path needed because there's nothing to migrate from.

The skeptical read: "post-quantum from day one" is still a marketing claim until adversarial cryptographers stress-test it. The optimistic read: if quantum timelines compress faster than BIP-360 and EIP-7560 can ship, first-mover advantage in post-quantum infrastructure is worth something real.


## Cambodia: Life in Prison for Crypto Scam Operators

Cambodia's National Assembly passed draft legislation targeting online scam operations with penalties up to life imprisonment for kingpins running large-scale fraud networks. All 112 present lawmakers backed it unanimously. The law still needs final approval before taking effect.

Cambodia hosts some of the world's most notorious crypto scam compounds - forced labor operations running "pig butchering" and romance scams at industrial scale. The law specifically targets the organizational layer, not just individual participants.

Experts quoted by ABC News are skeptical the crackdown will destroy the industry rather than displace it - Myanmar and Laos offer similar geography and weaker enforcement. The more meaningful signal is that Southeast Asian governments are treating crypto fraud as a serious organized crime problem rather than a tech regulation question. That changes the international cooperation calculus.

## Kentucky Self-Custody Win

Kentucky removed anti-self-custody provisions from its Blockchain Digital Asset Act. The specific provisions that were struck would have imposed burdensome requirements on individuals holding their own private keys - effectively treating self-custody wallets like licensed custodians.

This follows Alabama's SB 277 (DAO-like entities) and Arizona's SB 1649 (digital assets reserve). State-level crypto legislation is moving faster than federal regulation, and it's moving in a broadly pro-self-custody direction. The Kentucky win matters because self-custody provisions were being cited by other state legislatures as a model for restriction. That model is now off the table.


## MARA Cuts 15% of Staff After $1.1B BTC Sale

Bitcoin miner MARA Holdings has cut 15% of its full-time workforce across departments, with contractor reductions also expected. The cuts come shortly after MARA sold $1.1B worth of BTC as part of its "strategic evolution" from pure-play miner to energy and digital infrastructure company.

The framing is important: MARA is telling the market this is a pivot, not a retreat. Selling $1.1B in BTC while a Bitcoin miner is an unusual strategic move - it suggests management believes the mining revenue model is under structural pressure from halving economics and that diversification into energy infrastructure carries better long-term margins.

Whether that's correct or whether they're just diluting their BTC exposure at the worst possible time is the bet. At current difficulty levels and post-halving rewards, the economics of pure-play mining are genuinely challenging for all but the cheapest-power operators.
